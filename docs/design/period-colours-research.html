Tolstoy Period Colours — Research & Proposal

Last modified 19 April 2026 · view source

Design system research. Phase 1: Four base colours for the four periods of Tolstoy's life.
Last updated: 2026-04-19.

Confirmed Design Decisions (2026-04-19)

  • Four period colours approved as starting point: Green · Red · Amber · Blue
  • Role of colour: Visual flare only — accent and identity, never the sole carrier of navigation or interaction meaning. Colour is always accompanied by shape, label, or position.
  • Colour blindness: Johan has red-green deficiency (deuteranopia range). All period colour pairs must be testable by him directly. No design decision relying on red/green distinction will ship without his sign-off.
  • Background system: Both light and dark themes use the existing themeColor ramp in a heavily desaturated state — near-monochrome neutral fields. The four vivid period colours are applied as accents on top, giving them maximum pop against the quiet background. The theme colour unifies the two modes; the period colours provide the drama.
  • Colour pairing model: Each period colour will be matched and tuned to complement the theme colour, not just placed on top of it. The relationship between accent and ground is part of the design, not an afterthought.

The Four Periods

As Tolstoy describes in his preface to Birukoff's biography, his life falls into four natural periods:

# Period Years Character
I Youth & Formation 1828–1851 Childhood at Yasnaya Polyana, orphaned young, university dropout, moral diary-keeping, restless search for identity
II The Soldier & Sinner 1851–1862 Caucasus campaign, Sevastopol, Petersburg literary fame, gambling, brothels, travel in Europe — the period he later judged most harshly
III The Great Works 1862–1880 Marriage, Yasnaya Polyana school, War and Peace, Anna Karenina — world fame, yet the spiritual crisis already forming beneath
IV The Prophet 1880–1910 Conversion, renunciation of his earlier work, Christian anarchism, excommunication, the Tolstoyan movement, flight and death at Astapovo

Research: Colour Symbolism

Green — Youth & Formation

Your instinct: Green. This is strongly supported across every tradition consulted.

Western European tradition:
In medieval Europe green was the colour of youth, budding love, and growth — worn by lovers, associated with spring and the fertility of beginning things. It also carried a note of inexperience and instability, which maps well to this period: Tolstoy's youth was morally restless, not yet formed.

Russian Orthodox tradition:
Green is specifically the colour of eternal renovation, hope, flowering, youth, and nature. In Eastern Orthodox iconography it denotes where life begins — it appears in scenes of the Nativity and the Annunciation. For a project rooted in Russian culture, this is a particularly clean fit.

Literary tradition:
Green in European literature consistently represents growth, potential, and the unformed — what has not yet been tested by the world. Tolstoy's own diaries from this period obsessively track his moral failings and aspirations: a young man trying to become something.

Verdict: Green. Unanimous across traditions. Strong confidence.

Red — The Soldier & Sinner

Your instinct: "Some other colour" for the sinful period. The research points strongly to red.

Russian culture:
Red (krasny) in Russian means both red and beautiful — it is the colour of life, passion, fire, and sacrifice. It was the favoured colour of the Tsars, prominent in imperial architecture and military tradition. The 19th-century Russian military uniform was saturated with red and gold. This period is literally Tolstoy in uniform — Caucasus, Sevastopol, the Crimean War.

Western literary tradition:
Red is the primary colour of sin and passion in European literature — from The Scarlet Letter to Romantic poetry. It signals blood, desire, transgression, and the release of inhibitions. Tolstoy's own retrospective judgment on this period, in A Confession, is unsparing: gambling, sexual excess, violence, vanity. Red carries all of it.

Orthodox tradition:
Red symbolises life-giving energy, love, passion, and Christ's sacrifice — and the burning bush, where God's fire meets human encounter. There is a duality here: red is not simply sinful, it is intensely alive. The young Tolstoy was not yet dead to the world; he was burning in it.

Additional note — Amber/Orange:
One alternative worth considering is a deep amber or burnt orange — Tolstoy's famous krasny ugolok (beautiful corner) sensibility, and a colour associated with candlelight, the material world, earthly desire. But red is more historically grounded for the Russian military-literary context of this period.

Verdict: Red. Best fit across Russian cultural, military, and literary traditions. High confidence.

Amber/Gold — The Great Works

Your instinct: Uncertain. The research suggests amber or deep gold.

The case for gold:
In Orthodox tradition, gold is reserved for Christ and for divine light — it represents the uncreated light of God and is used for halos, backgrounds, and church domes. But in a secular design context, gold also carries meanings of achievement, mastery, and worldly glory — exactly what this period represents. Tolstoy at the height of his powers, producing works of world literature, celebrated across Europe, wealthy, famous.

The case for amber:
Amber is gold with warmth and depth — it evokes candlelight, harvest, autumn maturity, the richness of middle life. In the Russian context, amber is associated with the Baltic trade and with the cultivated interiors of estates like Yasnaya Polyana. It sits between the red of youth and the blue of the spiritual turn without being as ecclesiastically weighted as pure gold.

Literary/biographical resonance:
This is the period of War and Peace and Anna Karenina — immense, warm, novelistic human worlds. The colour of the period should feel rich and human, not cold. Gold/amber achieves this where, say, purple or yellow would not.

Verdict: Amber or deep gold. Recommend a warm, saturated amber — not a metallic gold, but a rich ochre-amber in the #C8860A–#D4A017 range. Medium-high confidence.

Blue — The Prophet

Your instinct: Blue — being in contact with God. The research fully confirms this.

Orthodox tradition:
Blue is unambiguously the colour of Heaven, the Mother of God, and divine transcendence in Eastern Orthodox iconography. Blue and white together are the colours of the Virgin Mary. Christ and the saints are depicted in blue robes as inhabitants of Heaven. In the iconostasis tradition that shaped Russian visual culture, blue is the colour of the world beyond the material.

Western Christian tradition:
From the 12th century, blue glass in Gothic cathedrals — the bleu de Saint-Denis — was understood to represent divine light. The Virgin Mary's blue robe in Western painting carries the same symbolism: purity, heaven, closeness to God. This usage was economically reinforced by the cost of lapis lazuli, making blue the colour of the spiritually and institutionally sacred.

German Romanticism:
The Blaue Blume (Blue Flower) of Novalis — a contemporary tradition Tolstoy would have known — represents the infinite longing for transcendence, for something beyond earthly reach. This resonates with the late Tolstoy's yearning quality, his rejection of everything worldly.

Biographical resonance:
The late Tolstoy was excommunicated by the Holy Synod — he was considered dangerous precisely because his spiritual claims were so absolute. He corresponded with Quakers, influenced Gandhi, wrote The Kingdom of God Is Within You. He was reaching toward something universal. Blue — the colour of heaven in every tradition he lived within — is exact.

Verdict: Blue. The most unambiguous choice of the four. Unanimous across Russian Orthodox, Western Christian, and literary traditions. Highest confidence.

Proposed Palette

These are starting points for refinement — vivid enough to distinguish clearly in a knowledge graph, distinct enough to carry semantic weight, harmonious enough to coexist.

Period Colour Proposed hex Quality
I — Youth & Formation Green #4A7C59 Forest green — young, alive, not yet bright
II — The Soldier & Sinner Red #B83232 Deep crimson — passionate, military, Russian
III — The Great Works Amber #C8860A Rich ochre-amber — warm, mature, worldly
IV — The Prophet Blue #2B5F8E Prussian-leaning blue — deep, spiritual, transcendent

Design considerations for the knowledge graph

  • All four colours should be tested at the same perceived luminance so no single period visually dominates the graph
  • For dark-background rendering (the main app and wiki), these base colours will need lighter tints — approx 60–70% lighter — for node fills, with the saturated base used for node borders or glow
  • For e-reader covers, the saturated base colours work well on white or cream backgrounds
  • The four colours should be distinct under colour-blindness simulation (deuteranopia most common — red/green confusion is the key risk here; the proposed red and green are sufficiently different in brightness and hue angle to remain distinguishable)

Colour blindness note

Johan has red-green colour deficiency himself and will be the primary reviewer for any red/green distinction problems. This is a significant advantage — issues will be caught before they ship rather than reported by users.

Non-negotiable rule: Colour is never the only signal. Every use of a period colour in the knowledge graph, timeline, or UI must be accompanied by at least one redundant encoding — shape, label, icon, or position. This applies globally across the design system, not just for accessibility compliance but because it makes the design more robust for everyone.

Open Questions for Johan

  1. Period II naming — "The Soldier & Sinner" follows Tolstoy's own retrospective framing in A Confession. Would you prefer a less judgemental label, e.g. "The Wandering Years" or "The World"?

  2. Gold vs Amber — Period III is the least settled. Do you want the ecclesiastical weight of gold (connecting to the Jubilee Edition, the canonical), or the warmer secular amber (connecting to the novels as human achievement)?

  3. Green shade — Forest green (#4A7C59) reads as mature and Russian. A brighter spring green (#5A9E6F) would read as younger but might feel too fresh/digital. Which direction?

  4. Knowledge graph design — Is the plan for the graph to be interactive (D3.js / WebGL) or a static illustration? This affects how the colours need to perform technically.
